Introduction

About this guide

This guide provides information about peer reviewing Red Hat product documentation.

The Red Hat Customer Content Services team created this guide as part of its responsibility for creating product documentation for Red Hat customers. Upstream communities who want to align more closely with the standards used by Red Hat product documentation can also use this guide.

Purpose of peer reviews

It’s recommended to perform a peer review on all updates to the documentation.

  • Peer reviews ensure a higher quality of our content which helps our users.

  • Reviewers should present their comments positively and avoid negative wording.

  • Authors must be open to reviewers' feedback. A second set of eyes can catch things that an author missed.

  • Peer reviews give both authors and reviewers a chance to see more content, find new ways to approach changes, and share expertise.

Providing feedback as a peer reviewer

Peer reviews should be kind, helpful, and consistent among peer reviewers.

  • Use a respectful tone in a peer review.

    • Support your suggested changes with documented resources such as style guides or Red Hat writing conventions.

    • Pose comments as questions when you are unsure.

    • Choose your wording carefully - do not be harsh. It is fine to be concise when it is a straightforward change. However, if it is a suggestion, ask them to “consider” your comment, or that you “suggest” something.

  • Stay within scope of reviewing new content, changed content, and the necessary context.

    • Review content that was changed in the pull request (PR) or merge request (MR).

    • Review the pre-existing section to ensure the new or updated content fits.

    • Do not request enhancements to the content unless the content is unclear without it.

    • If you notice an issue that was already in the existing content, use friendly wording such as “I know this was already there, but would you mind fixing that typo while you’re in there?”, or “I know this is out of scope for this PR, but in a future update maybe consider looking into this.” The author should either address it now, track it as a future request, or let the peer reviewer know that it is not something that they can address.

    • See Scope examples for more details on what is in or out of scope.

  • Differentiate between required and suggested changes.

    • Required changes must be fixed before the PR or MR can be merged. Support your change with a reference to the relevant style guide or principle. Examples: modular docs template adherence, typo fixes, following product-specific guidelines.

    • Suggested changes do not have to be addressed before the PR or MR can be merged. Use softer language, for example, “Here, it might be clearer to…​:” or a [SUGGESTION] tag to clearly indicate it to the author. Examples: wording improvements, content relocation, stylistic preference.

    • See Scope examples for more details on required versus suggested changes.

  • Understand that peer reviewers are not responsible for reviewing for technical accuracy.

    • SMEs and QE are responsible for testing and technical accuracy.

    • Peer reviewers check for things like usability issues and making sure that you can follow the steps in a procedure.

    • Peer reviewers do not need to question all technical details to understand everything themselves because the audience might be for users who are already familiar with the technology. Requests for additional technical information should be requested as a followup and not a requirement for the current PR.

    • However, some peer reviewers might be more familiar with a particular subject area or might know that an update might impact another area of the documentation. This is an appropriate situation to provide feedback on and explain your concern.

    • If you are certain that something is wrong or that a command will fail, ask the contributor to double check with their SME/QE. Avoid tagging their SMEs/QEs directly to ask.

  • Recognize that contributors do not have to accept all of your suggestions.

    • Some things are required, but other things are nice-to-haves. If it does not break any guidelines or is not an actual typo or issue, then you can often let authors keep it as it was.

    • If you are responsible for merging a PR or MR and feel strongly that the author should make a change, but they disagree, speak to the author in private. Cite style guides or vetted documentation so that they know your reasoning. Make sure that you listen to their perspective. If the topic of the disagreement is not in any of the guides, consider bringing it to the team for discussion. In some cases, the guidelines might need to be updated.

    • After completing your review, clearly mark it following your team’s best practice.

  • Add your own suggestions for improvements for a problematic area, rather than just “this doesn’t make sense”

    • Offer rewrites as suggestions; not something that the author has to take word-for-word. For example, “I don’t understand this description. Did you mean…​?”

    • Avoid rewriting entire paragraphs of the author’s content. If you find yourself doing this because there are multiple items in a paragraph that need attention, attempt to break out your suggestions. If you feel that providing an alternative paragraph wording is necessary, ensure that you make it clear that the author does not need to use your suggestion exactly.

    • If you notice a recurring issue, you can leave a global comment for the writer so that they know to address every instance of the issue. For example: "[GLOBAL] This typo occurs in other locations within the doc. I won’t comment on the other examples after this point but please address all instances."

  • If the review requires a significant amount of editing or rework, consider pausing the review and reaching out to the author directly to discuss.

    • This avoids overwhelming the author with too many comments and saves the peer reviewer’s time from reviewing content that might not be ready for peer review.

    • Examples of when to pause a review include if the build is broken, if the content is not rendering properly, or if the content is not modularized properly.

    • Reach out to the author privately, for example by chat, to express your concerns and provide advice on how to move forward.

    • Decide whether you have the bandwidth to work with the author, or if you need to request that they reach out to someone else, for example, an onboarding buddy or another senior writing member.

Peer review checklist

The peer review checklist can be used by both writers and peer reviewers as a quick reference to the Red Hat technical documentation style guidelines. You can use the checklist to help structure your peer reviews, and adapt the checklist to meet the needs of your team.

For guidance on each topic outlined in the checklist, see the following resources:

  • Red Hat supplementary style guide for product documentation

  • Minimalism in Red Hat documentation

  • Merriam-Webster Dictionary

Creating a peer review process

Red Hat Customer Content Services does not follow one definitive peer review process. Each team within CCS is different, depending on factors such as workflows, preferred tools, release cycles, engineering team preferences, and so on. Each team must determine a peer review process that works for them.

The most important thing is to define a process, so that peer reviews are used consistently throughout your team.

Considerations when creating a peer review process

You must consider many factors before you set up a peer review process so that the process meets the needs of your team.

Review the following factors:

  • Is a peer review required or optional?

  • Who are the peer reviewers?

  • How does a writer request a peer review?

  • How is the peer reviewer assigned?

  • What is the level or scope of the peer review?

  • Is there a checklist for the peer reviewer to follow?

  • What platform and tools are used to perform the review and give feedback?

  • What is the expected turnaround time?

  • How do urgent peer reviews get escalated?

  • How is peer review feedback incorporated?

Is a peer review required or optional?

Determine whether requesting a peer review is required or optional. A manager, Documentation Program Manager (DPM), or Content Stategist (CS) should communicate this expectation to the team.

Example options

  • A peer review is required on each GitHub pull request (or GitLab merge request) prior to accepting the request.

  • A peer review is only required in certain (defined) situations.

  • A peer review is optional.

Who are the peer reviewers?

Determine who is expected to peer review. A manager, DPM, or CS should communicate this expectation to the team.

Example options

  • Individuals can volunteer to be peer reviewers.

  • Everyone on the team is expected to be available to review at any time.

  • Everyone is expected to participate in peer reviews, and reviewers take turns being available.

How does a writer request a peer review?

Determine how writers request a peer review.

Example options

  • Add the request details to a tracking spreadsheet.

  • Ping a reviewer in a Google Chat or Slack channel.

  • Request a review through email.

  • Use GitHub/GitLab labels to mark when content is ready for review.

  • Open a Jira or Bugzilla with the request.

  • Ping a reviewer in the original documentation ticket.

How is the peer reviewer assigned?

Determine how peer reviewers are assigned to review content. Some methods might work better if the reviewers are on the same product team. Other methods might work better for cross-product reviews. Establish a method that suits the structure and dynamic of the group of writers and reviewers that the process targets.

Before a writer requests a peer review, the writer must ensure that the target peer review audience can access the peer review tracking mechanism and the content that requires a peer review. Otherwise, a peer reviewer cannot strictly follow the established peer review process for a product.

Example options

  • Reviewers check a tracking spreadsheet and assign themselves.

  • Peer reviewers are pinged with each request and assign themselves.

  • Reviewers regularly check a GitHub pull request or a GitLab merge request queue and assign themselves.

  • A writer contacts the peer reviewer.

What is the level or scope of the peer review?

Determine the level or scope of the peer review request so the writer and reviewer have the same expectations.

Note

The writer is responsible for informing the peer reviewer of any essential information related to the content.
Example options

  • Perform a basic typographic and link check.

  • Perform a full review of the content that includes checks on typographic errors and grammar, content placement or flow, structure, style guide compliance, and so on.

Is there a checklist for the peer reviewer to follow?

Determine what checklists and other resources the reviewer should follow.

Note

The writer is responsible for informing the peer reviewer of any essential information related to the content.
Example options

  • Follow the CCS peer review checklist.

  • Follow the CCS peer review checklist and a team-specific checklist.

What platform and tools are used to perform the review and give feedback?

Determine how to share content and how to provide feedback.

Example options

  • Draft content in a Google Doc and use the document for comments and suggestions.

  • Share a GitHub/GitLab pull request; reviewers can comment directly inline for each change.

  • Provide small snippets of content by email, instant messaging, or a ticket.

For more information, see Pros and cons of the different peer review platforms.

What is the expected turnaround time?

Determine the expected turnaround time for completing a peer review. Writers should communicate if there is any urgency or deadlines for the review.

Example options

  • Reviewers are expected to check the GitHub/GitLab queue daily or twice daily.

  • Peer reviewers are expected to respond to a Slack or Google Chat ping within a few hours.

  • Reviewers are expected to check the tracking spreadsheet daily.

  • Writers are expected to communicate the requested turnaround time after requesting the peer review.

How do urgent peer reviews get escalated?

Determine how an unassigned peer review request should be escalated if it could affect product release schedules.

Example options

  • Inform a manager, DPM, or CS of the unassigned time-critical peer review, so that they can escalate the peer review request or negotiate a new timeline for reviewing the content.

  • Use the peer review request channel that you established to request an urgent peer review. Ensure you detail the tight timelines in the channel, and assist a volunteer peer reviewer in a way that they can review the content with minimal distractions.

How is peer review feedback incorporated?

Determine the expectations for addressing or incorporating feedback. Expectations become important if the writer and peer reviewer disagree on a review item.

Incorporate an escalation process into your peer review process, such as requesting manager, DPM, or CS input or communicating in a guidelines group. This way, any disagreement between a writer and peer reviewer can be resolved.

Example options

  • Incorporate feedback at the writer’s discretion.

  • Establish a communication channel for informing the peer reviewer of the next steps.

  • Address peer review feedback and request the peer reviewer to perform a review of the revised content.

Finalizing your team’s peer review process

Both writers and peer reviewers must agree on the expectations for the peer review process.

Complete the following steps to finalize the peer review process:

  1. Draft a proposal for the peer review process.

  2. Share the proposal with the team and allow time for the team to provide feedback.

  3. Test the process to be sure that it works well for your team.

  4. Document the final process wherever your team stores its resources.

  5. Communicate the final process to the team and to any other contributors or stakeholders.

Example peer review process 1

The following diagram outlines an example process for a cross-product team that uses Jira tickets for communication and GitLab to perform peer reviews. This process requires that an SME review is completed first. The peer review squad consists of at least two members at any specific time. Membership of the squad rotates every week. The team maintains a peer review assignment roster in a Confluence page that lists the assigned reviewers for each week. The assignment roster is published in the Jira product dashboards, so that writers can see the assigned reviewers for the current week.

Writers submit requests for peer review by pinging the assigned reviewers in their Jira documentation tickets. The assigned reviewers agree among themselves who will perform each review. The reviewer performs the review in GitLab by submitting feedback on a merge request.

Appendix A: Pros and cons of the different peer review platforms

Review the following pros and cons for each platform to choose the right peer review method for your team.

GitHub or GitLab

Pros

  • Provides a convenient method for commenting on specific lines of content on a GitHub pull request (PR) or GitLab merge request (MR).

  • Includes functionality for easily adding additional reviewers.

  • Includes a mechanism for multiple people, such as writers, SMEs, or QEs, involved in a Jira ticket or Bugzilla ticket to collaborate on the same PR or MR.

  • Provides an easy linking functionality.

  • Offers the capability for writers to incorporate feedback before the PR or MR is approved.

Cons

  • Users must have familiarity with the GitHub or GitLab UI.

  • Users must have a login to comment on a PR or MR.

Google Docs

Pros

  • Includes a convenient method for commenting on specific text.

  • Includes functionality for easily adding additional reviewers.

  • Includes a mechanism for multiple people, such as writers, SMEs, or QEs, involved in a Jira ticket or Bugzilla ticket to collaborate on the same Google Doc.

  • Provides an easy linking functionality.

  • Allows you to copy and paste AsciiDoc content into a Google Doc.

Cons

  • Copying and pasting large chunks of content from HTML or PDF into a Google Doc can produce unreliable formatting.

  • Copying and pasting AsciiDoc content into a Google Doc can be time consuming.

  • Copying and pasting markup syntax into a Google Doc might lead to formatting issues for the pasted syntax.

Email

Pros

  • Email is an easy tool for anyone to use.

  • Messages provide an easy reference point for a writer.

Cons

  • Email capabilities make it difficult to link to specific email comments.

  • Email can be slow and time-consuming.

  • Feedback is difficult to comprehend if the content is not structured well.

IRC, Google chat, or Slack

Pros

  • Instant messaging is a fast communication tool.

  • Instant notifications are possible if a peer reviewer is online.

  • Instant messaging provides an opportunity for immediate discussion.

Cons

  • Participants must be online for IRC.

  • Message entry box has spatial limitations.

Jira or Bugzilla ticket

Pros

  • Multiple reviewers, such as a technical writer, peer reviewer, QE, and so on, can collaborate and confirm updates before any change is made.

  • Anyone following the ticket receives comments.

Cons

  • Submitted comments might be hard to edit.

  • Inline comments are hard to provide on the ticket.

  • Unwanted communications might result if too many users are following the ticket.

  • Lengthy content can be tedious to discuss with comments on a ticket.

  • Limited space makes it harder to add comments.
